Buyer's Guide — Gen 3 & Gen 4 (NM–NX)

4M41 6G75 2000–2021

What to check before buying a used Mitsubishi Pajero (Montero / Shogun) Gen 3 (2000–2006) or Gen 4 (2007–2021). Covers engine-specific issues, corrosion hotspots, 4WD system checks, recalls, and what to prioritise when inspecting any example.

Recalls — Check Before Buying

Run the vehicle's VIN through your country's official recall checker before viewing any car.
The following significant recalls have affected these vehicles:

Recall Models Affected Issue
Takata airbag inflator Various, including Gen 3 and some Gen 4 Inflator propellant degrades over time; risk of metal fragmentation on deployment. One of the largest automotive recalls in history. Verify completion.
Brake accumulator failure 2000–2003 Gen 3 Potential brake failure; approximately 13,400 vehicles affected. Accumulator replaced under recall.
Front lower control arm weld failure 2016–2018 Gen 4 Inadequate weld strength at the front right lower arm joint could cause the arm to break during use, compromising steering and stability. Arm replaced free of charge.

Do not purchase a vehicle with an outstanding Takata airbag recall. If the seller is unaware
of its status, treat this as a red flag about the ownership history. Check your country's
official recall database using the vehicle's VIN before viewing any car.

Engine: 3.2 DI-D Diesel (4M41)

The 3.2-litre diesel is the most common engine in the Pajero and is generally very durable.
Several specific issues warrant close attention.

Timing Chain — the Most Important Check

The upper plastic timing chain guide wears progressively and typically fails between
110,000–145,000 km (68,000–90,000 miles). When it breaks, fragments can jam the chain or
circulate in the oil, causing camshaft and lifter damage. This is the single most expensive
engine failure on these vehicles.

  • Early warning sign: A cold-start rattle from the top of the engine that settles after a
    few seconds of running.
  • Action: If the service history does not document chain guide replacement, budget for it
    immediately. Many specialists recommend replacing the guide proactively at 100,000 km (62,000 miles).
  • Ask specifically whether the chain, guides, and tensioner have been replaced as a set.

EGR Valve

Universal on higher-mileage examples, especially post-2006 models with a DPF. Carbon deposits
block the EGR valve and intake manifold, causing rough idle, lack of power, and increased smoke.
A clean or recently replaced EGR is a positive sign; if it has never been attended to past
100,000 km, budget for the job.

Injectors

Injectors typically run without issue until 150,000–200,000 km but can become varnished and
begin misfiring. The more serious risk on Gen 3 models is seized injectors — corrosion of the
copper seat caused by a rusty fuel filler neck can cause injectors to seize in the head.
Extraction without snapping the body requires specialist tooling. Check the fuel filler neck
condition carefully (see Underside & Corrosion below) as a proxy for injection system health.

Turbocharger

Check for worn shaft seals by removing the intercooler intake hose. Oily residue inside the
hose and intercooler indicates turbo seal wear. With the engine warm and at idle, look for blue
or grey smoke from the exhaust — persistent smoke is a concern. Check for excessive shaft play
at inspection.

Oil Cooler

The integrated oil cooler within the radiator can develop internal leaks, allowing coolant to
mix with engine oil. Check the dipstick and oil filler cap for creamy, mayonnaise-like
contamination. Any such contamination is a serious warning sign.

Glow Plugs and Relay

The glow plug relay is a common failure point. A faulty relay prevents the plugs from heating,
causing hard cold starting and white smoke on start-up. Individual plugs can seize in the head
on high-mileage engines, making extraction costly. Hard cold starting on a diesel is always
worth investigating.

Fuel Injection Pump

The high-pressure pump can fail due to contaminated fuel or neglected filter servicing.
Replacement is expensive. Running the tank very low or using poor-quality diesel accelerates
wear. Look for evidence of regular fuel filter changes in the service history.

Engine: 3.8 V6 Petrol (6G75 MIVEC)

Timing Chain and Hydraulic Tensioner

The 3.8 V6 uses a timing chain, not a belt. The plastic guides suffer the same wear pattern as
the diesel. Additionally, the hydraulic tensioner can bleed down or fail — if it does, a sudden
loud ticking or rattling results. Sudden loud ticking in a 3.8 MIVEC is a serious warning sign
requiring immediate investigation before chain skip causes valve damage.

MIVEC Variable Valve Timing System

The MIVEC system relies on clean oil passages and correct viscosity oil. Infrequent oil changes
cause clogged VVT actuator ports, resulting in hesitation, flat spots on acceleration, and
check-engine lights. A full service history using the correct oil grade is essential.

Oil Consumption

Valve stem seals show increased oil consumption past approximately 150,000 km. Check the
dipstick level at inspection and look for blue smoke on overrun or under hard acceleration.

Water Pump

Water pumps tend to fail after approximately 120,000 km. Check coolant condition at inspection
and look for weeping at the pump area on the front of the engine.

Bodywork

  • Verify the bonnet and fuel filler flap open correctly using the release levers under the
    steering column.
  • Check all four doors along the top of the door frame, where the metal is folded and
    joined. This seam is poorly sealed from the factory; rust begins here and spreads outward.
    Mitsubishi replaced door tops under warranty on some early Gen 4 cars. Look for paint
    bubbling along the top of all four doors.
  • Inspect wheel arch trims carefully. Rust develops behind the plastic arch covers;
    bubbling paint under the arch almost always indicates hidden corrosion.
  • Check the rear spare wheel carrier and its mounting frame on the rear door. These corrode
    heavily and replacement parts are becoming difficult to source.
  • Verify body panel alignment. Uneven gaps can indicate previous accident repairs.
  • On many models, the rear sidelights and indicators are located in the lower bumper lights
    rather than the main rear clusters.

Underside & Corrosion — Critical Inspection Areas

This is the most important inspection area on any used Pajero. Use a ramp wherever possible.

Fuel Filler Neck (Gen 3 Diesel — High Priority)

The fuel filler neck on 2000–2006 diesel models is concealed behind a plastic cover above the
right rear wheel. Mud, sand, and salt accumulate here and are rarely disturbed. The pipe
corrodes internally, and rust particles travel through the fuel system to the injection pump.
A visibly rusty filler neck is a direct warning sign for injection system damage. This is the
first thing to check on any Gen 3 diesel.

Chassis Rails — Front Radius Rod Mount (Safety Critical)

The front radius rod mounting point directly below the battery on the right chassis rail is a
severe corrosion point. Cases of the mount completely separating from the chassis rail have
been reported. This is a structural safety concern — inspect this area specifically and probe
with a screwdriver if any surface rust is visible.

Chassis and Sill Corrosion

  • Check along both full-length chassis rails for pitting and structural thinning.
  • Inspect the sill area behind plastic body cladding; remove mouldings at inspection where
    possible.
  • The area around the VIN stamping point on the right rear chassis is a common rust
    initiation point.

Rear Cargo Door Seal Area

Dirt and moisture collect under the tailgate rubber seal. Check the metal behind the seal
for corrosion.

Sunroof Drains (if sunroof fitted)

Blocked sunroof drain channels cause water to pool in the headlining and run into the A-pillar
cavity, causing corrosion and electrical issues. Ask whether the drains have ever been cleared.

Suspension Mounting Points

Check both front and rear suspension mounting points for corrosion, particularly around the
front upper strut towers and rear spring/shock mount areas.

Side Steps and Skid Plates

Verify the presence and condition of the factory bash plates/skid plates. Check side step
mounting points for rust. Confirm that the factory bottle jack, jack handle, and wheel brace
are present — missing factory equipment often indicates heavy use or poor ownership history.

Suspension

Upper Control Arm Bushings

The front upper control arm rubber bushings are a primary wear item on both Gen 3 and Gen 4.
The rubber separates from the steel sleeve. Symptoms are steering oscillation, wander, and a
clunking or knocking over bumps at low speed. Often worn through by 130,000 km (80,000 miles) on examples
used off-road or with heavy loads.

Lower Ball Joints

Lower ball joints are subject to high loads in off-road use. Wear produces clunking over bumps
and vibration through the steering wheel. Check at every pre-purchase inspection.

Radius Rod Rear Bush

A specific documented wear item: the rear bush of the front lower radius rod causes front-end
wander under braking when worn. Listen for movement and knock during hard braking.

Shock Absorbers

Check all four for oil leaks around the shaft seal. Bounce each corner — the vehicle should
settle in one oscillation, not continue bouncing.

Brakes

Brake caliper piston seizure is common on older examples.

  • Brake from 60 km/h — the vehicle should stop straight with no pulling, pulsing, or grinding.
  • Feel for excessive heat from any individual wheel after a moderate drive.
  • A warning buzzer during braking may indicate problems with the hydraulic brake booster motor
    or pressure accumulator.
  • An ABS warning light alone (with no other faults) is a common, typically inexpensive repair
    — often a faulty wheel speed sensor — but it must be scanned to confirm.

Wheels & Tyres

Factory wheel size is commonly 265/60R18. A common and fitment-compatible upgrade is
265/65R18.

All five tyres (including the spare) should match in size, brand, and wear level. Mismatched
sizes or significantly different wear patterns on an all-time 4WD can cause transmission and
transfer case wear. Check the spare is the same specification as the road wheels.

Super Select 4WD System

The Super Select transfer case is generally very robust, but lack of use causes problems with
actuators, vacuum solenoids, and position switches.

System modes:

  • 2H — rear-wheel drive only
  • 4H — full-time four-wheel drive (centre diff open)
  • 4HLc — full-time 4WD with centre differential locked
  • 4LLc — low range with centre differential locked

Checks during test drive:

  • Switch between 2H and 4H at moderate speed (30–40 km/h). The dashboard indicator should
    stop flashing and illuminate solidly within a few seconds. A mode that stays flashing
    indicates incomplete engagement.
  • Test all four modes in sequence. 4LLc requires a full stop.
  • After cycling through all modes, confirm no flashing differential warning lights remain.

The most common failure mode is the "dreaded flashing 4WD light" — the transfer case indicator
flashes and the mode does not engage. This can stem from actuator oil seal failure (gearbox oil
enters the actuator), vacuum leaks, or faulty position switches. Aftermarket replacement
switches are documented as slightly undersized compared to OEM, causing engagement failures.
Insist on OEM Mitsubishi parts for any switch replacement.

4HLc and 4LLc should only be used on loose or slippery surfaces. Using either mode on dry
tarmac at speed will cause transmission wind-up and drivetrain stress.

Transmission & Drivetrain

Automatic Gearbox

Gen 3 uses a 4-speed INVECS-II automatic; Gen 4 uses a Jatco-sourced 5-speed. Both require
regular ATF and filter changes — every 60,000 km. Check the service history specifically for
this. Incorrect ATF type (generic multi-spec fluid instead of genuine Mitsubishi ATF) is a
common cause of shudder and premature clutch pack wear.

During the test drive check for:

  • Smooth, firm changes under light and hard acceleration
  • No delayed engagement from Park to Drive or Reverse
  • No slipping between gears under load

CV Joints

Drive slowly in a tight circle in each direction and listen for clicking from the front wheels.
Any clicking on full lock requires investigation. Check front CV boots for splitting during the
underside inspection.

Electrical System

ETACS Control Unit

The ETACS (Electronic Time and Alarm Control System) is a yellow control module mounted under
the steering column. It controls indicators, lighting, wipers, central locking, and the
immobiliser. Failures cause:

  • Indicator malfunctions
  • Intermittent central locking faults
  • Immobiliser triggering unexpectedly, preventing starting

ETACS replacement requires a Mitsubishi MUT-III dealer diagnostic tool to program the new unit
and register keys. It cannot be swapped without reprogramming. If a vehicle has unexplained
immobiliser issues or a history of phantom electrical faults, get a dedicated Mitsubishi scanner
on it before buying.

Multiple Warning Lights Simultaneously

ASC, traction control, centre differential lock, and engine warning lights coming on together
usually indicates a sensor communication fault or grounding issue rather than multiple
individual component failures. A common cause is the wiring loom under the gearbox tunnel
working loose from its retaining clips and contacting the front driveshaft — inspect that loom
during the underside check. Any multi-warning-light car must be diagnosed with a scan tool
before purchase.

Steering Column Controls (Clockspring)

Test every steering wheel button individually — horn, audio controls, cruise control.
Intermittent or non-functioning buttons indicate a failing clockspring (spiral cable).

Electric Windows

Test all windows through their full travel in both directions. Slow or jerky operation
indicates a worn regulator. A window that stops mid-travel suggests a motor or fuse issue.

Interior

  • Test all seat adjustment mechanisms. Confirm second-row seats fold and slide correctly.
  • Confirm the infotainment system powers on and functions correctly.
  • Test the centre armrest compartment and its sliding lid.
  • Verify the handbrake holds the vehicle securely in neutral on a slope.
  • Check the third-row seats and headrests are present (where applicable); boot trim panels and
    hinge covers should also be in place.

Standard Equipment Checklist

Confirm the vehicle includes:

  • Factory bottle jack, handle, and wheel brace
  • Locking wheel nut key
  • Removable tow bar and electrics (if originally fitted)
  • Third-row seats and headrests (where applicable)
  • All boot trim panels and hinge covers

Missing factory equipment often indicates heavy use or poor ownership history.

Test Drive

A healthy vehicle should:

  • Start quickly with no excessive smoke
  • Shift gears smoothly at all speeds
  • Drive quietly without suspension knocks or creaks
  • Reach normal operating temperature without overheating
  • Engage all drivetrain modes correctly

A slight knocking sensation under the driver's feet with a nearly full fuel tank can be normal
due to fuel tank shape and fuel movement. Manual shift mode (if fitted) should operate
correctly when selected.

Servicing & Maintenance

Recommended service intervals:

  • Every 12 months or every 20,000 km (12,500 miles) (whichever comes first)
  • Valve clearance check: every 40,000 km (25,000 miles) — 4M41 diesel
  • Automatic transmission fluid and filter: every 60,000 km (37,000 miles)
  • Timing chain guide (4M41 diesel): replacement recommended proactively at 100,000 km (62,000 miles)

A strong, documented service history is significantly more important than mileage. A
high-mileage car with full history is almost always a better buy than a lower-mileage car
with no documented maintenance.

Coolant: Mitsubishi specifies blue HOAT coolant. Orange, green, or brown coolant in the
reservoir indicates incorrect mixing and likely accelerated internal corrosion.

Summary: What to Prioritise

  1. Check your country's recall database before viewing — non-negotiable for Takata airbag
    status
  2. Inspect the fuel filler neck and chassis rail at the battery on any Gen 3 diesel
  3. Ask for timing chain guide history — the single most expensive engine repair
  4. Test all four 4WD modes and confirm clean engagement
  5. Check the ATF service history — a never-serviced automatic is a known failure point
  6. Drive over bumps at low speed and listen for suspension clunks
  7. Run a diagnostic scan on any car with warning lights, electrical oddities, or unknown
    history

A clean Gen 4 with full service history and a recently replaced timing chain guide is worth
a meaningful premium. Walk away from any vehicle with structural chassis corrosion, milky oil,
or an unresolved Takata recall.
