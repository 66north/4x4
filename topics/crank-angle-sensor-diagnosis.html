Crank Angle Sensor — Diagnosis & Replacement

● Moderate ⏱ 60 min Gen 4 (2007–2013) 4M41 6G75 2008–2013

The crank angle sensor (CAS) reads the crankshaft position and speed, providing the ECU with timing information for fuel injection and ignition. A failed CAS causes immediate no-start or stalling, as the ECU cannot determine when to fire injectors or ignition. Unlike oxygen sensors that degrade gradually, the CAS either works or doesn't — there is no "weak" state. Symptoms are sudden, not progressive. The sensor is located on the engine block near the crankshaft pulley and can be tested with a multimeter and an oscilloscope (workshop tools).

When do you need this?

  • Engine will not start (cranks but no ignition or fuel)
  • Engine dies immediately after starting (cannot maintain sync)
  • Stalling at random speeds or idle (intermittent sensor signal loss)
  • Check Engine light with code P0335 (Crankshaft Position Sensor Circuit)
  • Check Engine light with code P0336 (Crankshaft Position Sensor Signal Out of Range)
  • Rough idle or hesitation on acceleration (weak or unstable signal)

Tools needed

  • Multimeter (to test sensor output voltage)
  • Oscilloscope (for detailed waveform analysis — workshop tool)
  • 10mm or 12mm socket (sensor mounting bolt)
  • OBD-II scanner (to read fault codes and sensor data)

Tips & common mistakes

  • The CAS is tested by cranking the engine and measuring AC voltage at the sensor connector (usually 0.3–2V AC, depending on engine speed). If cranking produces zero voltage, the sensor is dead.
  • Do not test with the key ON (engine off) — the sensor produces no signal without crankshaft rotation. Always crank the engine to see a signal.
  • If the multimeter shows AC voltage but the engine still won't start, the problem is not the CAS — the issue is in the wiring to the ECU or the ECU itself.
  • A corroded sensor connector is a common cause of intermittent no-start. Check the connector at the sensor and at the ECU. Clean terminals with electrical contact cleaner and apply dielectric grease.
  • The sensor gap (distance from the sensor tip to the crankshaft timing ring) is critical: typically 0.3–0.5 mm. If the sensor was removed during another repair and reinstalled too far away, it will not read the crankshaft signal. Check gap before condemning a new sensor.
  • If replacing the CAS, mark the original sensor's angular position (e.g., pointing at the 12 o'clock position on the block) before removal. Reinstall the new sensor in the same position to avoid further timing issues.
  • After replacing the CAS, clear the Check Engine codes and drive a full cycle (cold start, warm-up, highway speeds) to allow the ECU to relearn. The fault may re-appear if timing is not correct.
