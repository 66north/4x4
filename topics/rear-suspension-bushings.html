Rear Suspension Bushings & Ball Joints

● Intermediate ⏱ 120 min Gen 4 (2007–2013) 4M41 6G75 2008–2013

The Pajero IV rear suspension uses a multi-link arrangement with trailing arms, toe control arms, upper arms, and a stabiliser bar — all connected via rubber bushings and ball joints. Worn bushings cause vague handling, rear-end wandering, and clunking over bumps. Ball joint wear causes play and can be detected by lifting the wheel and checking for movement. Bushing replacement requires pressing out the old rubber and pressing in new ones, which typically needs a hydraulic press — this is a workshop job, but inspection is straightforward.

When do you need this?

  • Clunking or knocking from the rear over bumps or during hard braking
  • Rear-end wander or loose feeling on straight roads
  • Uneven rear tyre wear (worn bushings change toe angle)
  • Squeaking from the rear suspension during slow manoeuvring
  • Visible cracking or splitting in rubber bushings (inspect during service)
  • Play detected when lifting the rear wheel and pushing/pulling the tyre

Tools needed

  • Jack and axle stands
  • 17mm socket (wheel bolts)
  • Pry bar (to check ball joint play with wheel loaded)
  • Ball joint separator (pickle fork) for removal
  • Hydraulic press (for bushing replacement — workshop tool)
  • Torque wrench

Torque specifications

  • Trailing arm to body mount bolt: 120 N·m — Tighten with suspension at ride height (vehicle weight on wheels), not at full droop.
  • Toe control arm ball joint nut: 98 N·m — 95–100 Nm. Always use new nut — these are self-locking.
  • Stabiliser link ball joint nut: 44 N·m — 40–50 Nm.

Tips & common mistakes

  • All suspension arm bolts must be torqued with the vehicle at ride height (on the ground or on a ramp with the suspension loaded). Torquing at full droop pre-loads the bushings and causes premature failure.
  • Bushing inspection: look for visible rubber cracking, collapse (the bush has sagged and there is a gap around it), or separation (the inner metal sleeve is moving independently). Any of these means replacement.
  • Ball joint play check: lift the rear axle (not individual wheels) so both rear wheels hang freely. Grip each tyre at 12 and 6 o'clock and push/pull — check for movement in the toe arm joint. Grip at 3 and 9 o'clock to check the upper arm joint.
  • Stabiliser link turning torque: can be checked with a torque wrench — the ball joint should rotate with a specific resistance (typically 0.5–3.0 Nm). Zero resistance means the ball has worn loose.
  • Rear wheel alignment: whenever any rear suspension component is replaced, a four-wheel alignment is required. Rear toe and camber are adjustable on the Pajero IV and shift when arms are disturbed.
  • Polyurethane bushing upgrades are popular for off-road use — they resist oil contamination and last longer than rubber. However, they transmit more noise and vibration into the cabin. Use standard rubber for a daily driver.
